Projects — Medical

A fibre the body accepts — then rebuilds around

Two active programmes apply natural spider silk to problems where today's materials fall short: anterior cruciate ligament reconstruction, and peripheral nerve repair.

Programme 01

The ACL scaffold

The clinical problem. Anterior cruciate ligament injuries affect millions of people every year. Current reconstruction relies on autografts — harvesting the patient's own tendon — or synthetic materials, each with real limitations in donor-site morbidity, integration, and long-term durability.

The candidate material. Spider silk's tensile strength exceeds most synthetics, it is biocompatible, and it degrades predictably as the body builds new tissue around it. Critically, it can be fabricated into scaffold geometries that mimic the fibre architecture of a native ligament.

Where the research stands

The programme is in the feasibility-study phase with the research team, establishing scaffold fabrication and mechanical characterisation against native-ligament benchmarks. Key milestones to date include material selection and braiding geometry; the next steps move toward in vivo testing.

Programme 02

The nerve conduit

The clinical problem. Peripheral nerve injuries often cause permanent loss of function, because the body's natural regeneration capacity is limited over longer gaps. A nerve conduit bridges that gap and guides axonal regrowth across it.

The rationale. Spider silk can be spun into tubular architectures with controlled porosity and surface chemistry that supports Schwann-cell adhesion — the cellular scaffolding that healthy nerve regrowth depends on.

Stage & pathway. The work is at the early scientific-investigation stage, conducted with academic partners. The regulatory pathway follows the established route for resorbable nerve-repair devices, staged from pre-clinical evidence toward first-in-human study.
